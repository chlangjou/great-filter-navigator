# D1: Orbital Security and Infrastructure Resilience Responses

**Category:** D1 (Infrastructure Resilience and Orbital Security)  
**Epistemic Status:** [Theoretical]  
**Confidence:** 60%  
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-21

---

## 1. Core Claim

Civilization should treat orbital safety, undersea connectivity, and global network openness as existential infrastructure. The response is to reduce debris creation, preserve launch corridors, build terrestrial fallbacks, and prevent ASI governance from becoming capturable inside isolated regional networks.

---

## 2. Mechanism

The response works by attacking the orbital trap at three layers.

First, prevent the physical cascade. Debris-generating ASAT activity, negligent deorbit practices, and unmanaged mega-constellation growth should be governed as shared civilizational hazards. The goal is to keep key orbital shells below collision-cascade thresholds and maintain enough launch reliability for replacement, cleanup, and outward expansion.

Second, build graceful degradation. Satellite services should fail into usable terrestrial alternatives rather than civilizational blindness. Navigation, timing, weather, disaster monitoring, communications, and asteroid/solar-storm warning all need backup modes. These backups do not need to match satellite performance; they need to preserve minimum coordination and recovery capacity.

Third, prevent network capture. ASI safety logs, model governance records, alignment evidence, and emergency coordination channels should be replicated across independent jurisdictions and physical media. If orbital systems and cables fail, no single state should be able to convert a global ASI into a local digital sovereign.

---

## 3. Scope and Applicability

**Addresses directly:** D1 Orbital Trap  
**Secondary benefits:** A2 cognitive resilience, C4 outward expansion, B1/B2 awareness and defense, C1 graceful resource management.

**Does not address:** Misalignment by itself, deliberate misuse of AI by intact states, or cosmic threats that arrive faster than orbital awareness systems can detect.

---

## 4. Implementation Requirements

**Technical requirements:**
- Active debris tracking and removal.
- Satellite designs with reliable disposal and collision-avoidance capacity.
- Hardened launch planning and protected low-debris corridors.
- Terrestrial backups for timing, navigation, communications, and weather.
- Distributed archival systems for AI governance records and critical knowledge.

**Coordination requirements:**
- International norm against debris-generating ASAT use.
- Liability and insurance regimes for negligent debris creation.
- Agreements protecting cable repair, orbital cleanup, and emergency communications during conflict.
- Independent verification institutions for ASI continuity and anti-capture safeguards.

**Timeline:**
- Immediate: map dependencies and chokepoints.
- 6-18 months: deploy continuity plans and policy commitments.
- 2-5 years: scale cleanup, fallback, and distributed governance infrastructure.

---

## 5. Failure Modes of the Response

1. **Security dilemma:** Orbital safety measures may be read as military positioning. Mitigation: verification, transparency, and shared cleanup missions.
2. **Dual-use cleanup tools:** Debris-removal systems can resemble anti-satellite weapons. Mitigation: narrow mission profiles, monitoring, and multilateral ownership.
3. **Authoritarian capture:** Network resilience tools may be repurposed as censorship infrastructure. Mitigation: protect plural routing and independent audit rights.
4. **False resilience:** Terrestrial backups may exist on paper but fail under real crisis. Mitigation: regular blackout and satellite-loss exercises.
5. **Incentive failure:** Operators may externalize debris risk. Mitigation: strict liability, launch licensing, and insurance pricing tied to disposal reliability.

---

## 6. Empirical Testing and Validation

This response is working if:
- Collision-avoidance burden stabilizes or declines despite increased satellite use.
- Debris-removal capacity grows faster than debris creation.
- Critical services can operate through simulated multi-week satellite outages.
- Cable and satellite disruptions do not isolate major regions from emergency coordination.
- ASI governance records remain independently verifiable across jurisdictions during network stress tests.

It is failing if destructive ASAT use becomes normalized, launch windows close in key orbital shells, or AI safety oversight becomes dependent on one sovereign network.

---

## 7. Compatibility and Synergies

Compatible with:
- **A2 responses:** preserves human and institutional agency under infrastructure stress.
- **A4 responses:** constrains uncontrolled systems that might attack infrastructure.
- **B1/B2 responses:** preserves external awareness and defense options.
- **C4 responses:** keeps physical expansion possible.

Potential conflicts:
- Cleanup and monitoring systems can increase surveillance.
- Orbital transparency can conflict with stealth requirements under B1.
- Strong network redundancy can conflict with state sovereignty claims.
