## Case 2: COVID-19 as Near-Miss Great Filter

**What is it:** Global pandemic that killed millions but didn't cause civilization collapse

**Framework Mapping:**
- **Relates to:** C2 (Anthropic Shadow), near-miss detection
- **Framework Prediction:** Civilization regularly encounters near-extinction events; we notice only the near-misses
- **Navigator says:** Should expect close calls; they indicate thin survival branches

**Reality Check:**
‚úÖ **What the framework explains well:**
- Why pandemic was simultaneously deadly and non-civilizational
- Why it felt like "worst-case" but civilization adapted
- Why response capacity (health systems, coordination) mattered critically

‚ö†Ô∏è **What the framework partially explains:**
- Economic and social fragility revealed
- International coordination breakdowns observed
- Why we got "lucky" with variant trajectories

‚ùå **What the framework doesn't explain:**
- Whether COVID was actually an existential threat (probably not by strict definition)
- Role of chance vs. built capability in survival
- Why this specific threat triggered while others didn't

**Conclusion:** Framework correctly identifies this as a "warning" but may overestimate its significance as a Great Filter test.

## Case 3: AI Alignment Research Investment

**What is it:** Growing but still-limited funding for AI alignment research relative to overall AI spending

**Framework Mapping:**
- **Relates to:** A1 (Alignment Disconnect), C1 (Resource allocation)
- **Framework Prediction:** Civilization should be investing heavily in alignment work; underfunding indicates dangerous misalignment of priorities
- **Navigator says:** Resource allocation failure on existential risks

**Reality Check:**
‚úÖ **What the framework explains well:**
- Why A1 mechanisms matter (alignment does get harder with capability)
- Why early investment is critical
- Why coordination failure on priorities is dangerous

‚ö†Ô∏è **What the framework partially explains:**
- Why some organizations prioritize alignment (they do, increasing)
- Market mechanisms for funding existential risk work
- Implicit discounting of tail risks in economic decision-making

‚ùå **What the framework doesn't explain:**
- Epistemic uncertainty about whether alignment is actually solvable
- Opportunity cost tradeoffs (what gets sacrificed for alignment focus?)
- Why different organizations rationally prioritize differently

**Conclusion:** Framework correctly identifies the risk but may underestimate rational uncertainty about solutions.

## Framework Limitations Identified

### 1. **Technical Feasibility**
Framework proposes responses (e.g., stealth protocols, verification systems) but doesn't always address:
- Whether they're actually technically achievable
- What physical constraints prevent them
- Whether they scale to civilizational levels

### 2. **Incentive Alignment**
Framework assumes coordination is possible but doesn't always address:
- Why nations/actors would cooperate
- What defection incentives exist
- How to align private interests with civilizational safety

### 3. **Epistemological Humility**
Framework sometimes speaks with more confidence than warranted:
- Some hypotheses are highly speculative (B3, B4)
- Uncertainty ranges not always quantified
- Alternative explanations not always considered

### 4. **Time Scales**
Framework sometimes mismatches real timescales:
- Political cycles vs. existential timescales
- Human attention spans vs. long-term risks
- Technological development speed vs. coordination speed
