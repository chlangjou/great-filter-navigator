## 1. Measurement Definition

### What We're Tracking
The **Cognitive Resilience Metric** measures civilization's ability to:
- Maintain essential functions without AI assistance
- Recover from AI system failures
- Adapt to unexpected scenarios
- Solve novel problems without delegating to AI

### The Core Metric
```
Cognitive_Resilience = (Functions_Viable_Without_AI / Total_Critical_Functions) Ã— 
                       (Human_Problem_Solving_Capacity / Required_Capacity)
```

**Assessment:**
- **Resilience > 0.7** = Civilization could function effectively even with major AI failures (NOMINAL)
- **Resilience 0.3-0.7** = Partial AI failure would cause significant disruption; recovery possible (CAUTION)
- **Resilience < 0.3** = Civilization cannot function without AI; failure is catastrophic (CRITICAL)

### Why This Matters
Cognitive resilience is the "buffer" that prevents a single AI failure from cascading into civilizational collapse. High resilience means humans can think and act independently.

## 4. Interpretation Guidance

### High Resilience (NOMINAL)
- **Meaning**: Civilization can function independently; AI is a performance multiplier, not a requirement
- **Implication**: Civilization has optionality and flexibility; can weather AI failures
- **Action**: Maintain human capacity; continue independent problem-solving capability

### Moderate Resilience (CAUTION)
- **Meaning**: Civilization could function without AI but would experience significant disruption
- **Implication**: AI is becoming important for normal function; dependence growing
- **Actions**: 
  - Maintain critical human skills and knowledge
  - Protect workforce from de-skilling through overuse of AI
  - Create redundancy in critical problem-solving capabilities
  - Resist trend toward increasing complexity beyond human capacity

### Low Resilience (CRITICAL)
- **Meaning**: Civilization cannot function without AI; failure is existential
- **Implication**: Civilization is highly fragile; vulnerable to single points of failure
- **Actions**: Trigger A2 emergency protocols
  - Immediately implement human skill restoration programs
  - Reduce problem complexity or break into human-solvable components
  - Create distributed problem-solving capability
  - Implement AI redundancy and failover systems

## 5. Failure Modes & Limitations

### How This Indicator Could Be Misread

**False Positive Risk:**
- *Optimistic retrospection*: Assuming humans could handle pre-AI problems when many involved lucky circumstances
- *Inflation of human capacity*: Overestimating what humans can actually accomplish
- *Ignoring scale*: Problems were simpler when civilization was smaller

**False Negative Risk:**
- *Normalization of dependence*: Not noticing that problems have become incomprehensible to new generations
- *Hidden knowledge loss*: Critical expertise exists in older workers; younger generation has not learned it
- *Fragile redundancy*: Humans could theoretically solve problems but lack time, resources, or motivation to do so

### How to Mitigate
1. **Test in simulation**: Actually practice AI-off scenarios, don't just assume capability
2. **Cross-generational assessment**: Check that younger cohorts have equivalent problem-solving capacity
3. **Monitor complexity trends**: Track whether problems are becoming harder faster than capacity increases
4. **Maintain diversity**: Ensure multiple independent groups can solve critical problems
5. **Require active skill use**: Don't let critical skills atrophy; integrate into regular work
